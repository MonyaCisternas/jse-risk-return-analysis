Portfolio Risk–Return Analysis of Selected JSE Stocks¶

Author: Monya Cisternas
Date: February 2026
Objective: Evaluate historical performance and identify the optimal risk-adjusted portfolio allocation.

Executive Summary¶

This analysis evaluates five JSE-listed equities between 2018 and 2025 to assess return, volatility, and diversification characteristics.

Key objectives:

  • Compare individual stock performance
  • Measure risk using standard deviation
  • Analyse correlations between assets
  • Simulate portfolio allocations
  • Identify the portfolio with the highest Sharpe ratio

The goal is to determine whether diversification improves risk-adjusted returns relative to holding a single asset.

Summary of Findings¶

  • SHP.JO delivered the strongest risk-adjusted performance
  • SOL.JO exhibited highly disproportionate volatility
  • Optimal portfolio heavily weighs on SHP.JO due to its superior risk-adjusted profile and allocates minimal capital to SOL.JO given its high risk and low return contribution.
  • Max Sharpe portfolio: 19.3% return, 24% volatility

Business Problem¶

Investors must balance expected return against volatility.
Holding a single high-return stock may expose an investor to excessive risk.

This analysis explores whether combining assets across sectors (financials, retail, resources, and global tech exposure) improves portfolio stability and maximises risk-adjusted performance.

Average Annual Returns¶

The average annual returns indicate differences in long-term performance across the selected JSE stocks.

  • SHP.JO (21.33%) delivered the highest average annual return, suggesting strong historical growth over the selected period.
  • AGL.JO (17.78%) demonstrated solid performance, likely reflecting its exposure to global commodity cycles.
  • PRX.JO (13.97%) showed moderate growth that is consistent with its international tech exposure.
  • FSR.JO (9.61%) produced steady but comparatively lower returns, typical of a large financial institution.
  • SOL.JO (1.91%) underperformed significantly compared to other assets. This indicates weak historical growth during the selected period.

Retail and resource exposure from SHP.JO and AGL.JO appear to have generated the strongest returns, while energy (SOL.JO) contributed the least.

Annual Volatility¶

  • SOL.JO (70.45%) shows extremely high volatility, indicating significant price fluctuations and high risk exposure.
  • AGL.JO (41.33%) and PRX.JO (40.48%) show relatively high risk, reflecting sensitivity to global commodity and tech market movements.
  • FSR.JO (33.36%) reflects moderate risk, consistent with the exposure in large-cap financial sectors.
  • SHP.JO (29.84%) exhibits stable price behaviour, as shown in its lower volatility.

Financial and retail exposures provide lower risk profiles compared to energy and resource-linked assets.

Risk/Return Comparative Analysis¶

A comparison of annualised volatility and returns to identify attractiveness of selected stocks.

  • SHP.JO (Return: 21.33%, Risk: 29.84%) presents the most attractive standalone profile with the highest return and lowest volatility.
  • AGL.JO (Return: 17.78%, Risk: 41.33%) materially higher risk with solid return potential.
  • PRX.JO (Return: 13.97%, Risk: 40.48%) elevated risk with moderate growth potential.
  • FSR.JO (Return: 9.61%, Risk: 33.36%) moderate risk with steady, although lower growth.
  • SOL.JO (Return: 1.91%, Risk: 70.45%) minimal return with severely high risk. This profile is the least attractive.

Key Observations¶

  • SHP.JO is the most efficient asset over the selected period with an attractive risk/return ratio.
  • SOL.JO increases portfolio risk significantly without a proportionate return contribution.
  • Diversification benefits will depend on correlation structure rather than return alone.

Although individual asset performance is informative, an optimal portfolio construction requires analysing correlation and covariance to determine whether diversification can reduce risks and optimise returns.

Conclusion¶

This analysis evaluated the historical performance of five JSE-listed equities between 2018 and 2025 using modern portfolio theory principles. By comparing annualised returns, volatility, correlations, and simulated portfolio allocations, several key insights emerged.

SHP.JO demonstrated the strongest individual risk–return profile, combining high returns with comparatively low risk. In contrast, SOL.JO exhibited high volatility with very low return contribution, making it less attractive on a risk-adjusted basis.

The Monte Carlo simulation of 5,000 random portfolios revealed that the maximum Sharpe ratio portfolio achieved an expected annual return of approximately 19% with volatility near 24%. The optimal portfolio allocated the majority of capital to SHP.JO, reflecting its superior risk-adjusted characteristics, while assigning minimal weight to higher-volatility, lower-efficiency assets.

This outcome reinforces a core principle of portfolio theory: optimal asset allocation depends not only on individual returns but on covariance structure and diversification benefits. Assets that improve overall portfolio efficiency receive higher weight, even if they are not the absolute highest-return assets.

Limitations¶

This analysis relies on historical data and assumes return distributions remain stable over time. Market regimes, macroeconomic conditions, and structural changes may alter future performance. Additionally, transaction costs, taxes, and liquidity constraints were not accounted for in the simulation.

Final Insight¶

The project demonstrates that disciplined quantitative analysis can transform raw market data into actionable portfolio insights. By focusing on risk-adjusted returns and diversification, investors can construct portfolios that are more efficient than any individual asset.
